# Electoral System of France: The Two-Round System (TRS)

France uses a **Two-Round System** for its most important elections. This ensures that the eventual winner has a broad base of support and prevents "accidental" winners in a crowded field.

## 1. Core Mechanics

### Round 1: The Qualifier
All candidates who meet the entry requirements appear on the ballot. If a candidate receives an absolute majority (**50% + 1 vote**) of the valid votes cast (and in legislative elections, at least 25% of registered voters), they are elected immediately.

### Round 2: The Runoff
If no one wins in Round 1, a second round is held (usually two weeks later):
*   **Presidential:** Only the **top two** candidates proceed to the second round.
*   **Legislative (National Assembly):** Anyone who received at least **12.5% of the total registered voters** in the first round can proceed.

---

## 2. Illustrated Example: Legislative Election

**Scenario:** A constituency with 50,000 registered voters. The turnout is 60% (30,000 votes cast).

### Round 1 Results
*   **Candidate A (Hard Right):** 11,000 votes (36.7%)
*   **Candidate B (Left):** 9,000 votes (30.0%)
*   **Candidate C (Center):** 7,000 votes (23.3%)
*   **Others:** 3,000 votes

**Who qualifies for Round 2?**
The threshold is 12.5% of *registered* voters: $50,000 \times 0.125 = \mathbf{6,250}$.
*   Candidate A (11,000), B (9,000), and C (7,000) all beat the threshold.
*   This creates a **"Triangular" (3-way) contest**.

### The "Republican Front" Strategy
In many French elections, if a "triangular" includes a candidate from an extremist party (e.g., Candidate A), the third-place candidate (Candidate C) might **withdraw** to allow their voters to consolidate behind the other mainstream candidate (Candidate B) to "block" the extremist.

### Round 2 Results (After Candidate C Withdraws)
*   **Candidate B (Left):** 17,000 votes (**WINNER**)
*   **Candidate A (Hard Right):** 13,000 votes

---

## 3. Key Characteristics
*   **Mandate:** The winner in the second round usually has a clear majority of the votes cast.
*   **Coalition Building:** The two weeks between rounds are a time of intense political negotiation.
*   **Negative Voting:** Many voters use the second round to "vote against" the candidate they dislike the most, rather than "for" their favorite.
