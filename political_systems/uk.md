# Electoral System of the United Kingdom: First-Past-The-Post (FPTP)

The UK uses the **First-Past-The-Post (FPTP)** system for general elections to the House of Commons. It is a "plurality" system, meaning the winner is the candidate with the most votes, regardless of whether they have a majority.

## 1. Core Mechanics

### Constituencies
The UK is divided into **650 geographic areas** called constituencies. Each constituency elects exactly one Member of Parliament (MP).

### One Vote
Each voter casts a single vote for one candidate in their local constituency.

### The Winner
The candidate who receives more votes than any other individual candidate is elected. There is no second round and no ranking of preferences.

---

## 2. Illustrated Example: The "Winner's Bonus" and "Wasted Votes"

### Local Level: The "Plurality" Winner
Imagine a constituency called "Oxbridge West" with 10,000 voters.

| Candidate | Party | Votes | Result |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Alice** | **Progressive** | **3,600** | **WINNER** |
| Bob | Conservative | 3,400 | Lost |
| Charlie | Liberal | 2,000 | Lost |
| Dana | Independent | 1,000 | Lost |

**Analysis:**
*   Alice wins the seat with only **36%** of the vote.
*   **6,400 votes (64%)** were cast for candidates who did not win. These are often called "wasted votes" because they do not contribute to any representation.

### National Level: The Disproportionate Result
FPTP often creates a "winner's bonus," where a small lead in votes translates into a massive majority in seats.

| Party | National Vote % | Seats Won | Seat % |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Party A** | 38% | 350 | 54% |
| **Party B** | 33% | 200 | 31% |
| **Party C** | 15% | 12 | 2% |

**Analysis:**
*   **Party A** gets a governing majority despite 62% of the country voting for someone else.
*   **Party C** has significant support but because its voters are spread out geographically, they rarely come first in any single constituency, resulting in very few seats.

---

## 3. Key Characteristics
*   **Strong Link:** Voters know exactly who their local MP is.
*   **Stability:** Usually results in a single party having enough seats to form a government without a coalition.
*   **Tactical Voting:** Voters often vote for their "second favorite" if their favorite candidate has no chance of winning in that specific area.
