# Electoral System of Italy: The Rosatellum

The **Rosatellum** (named after its creator Ettore Rosato) is Italy's current mixed electoral system. It is a "parallel" system, meaning it uses two different methods of election at the same time, but they do not influence each other's seat counts.

## 1. Core Mechanics

### The Seat Split
Seats in the Chamber of Deputies and the Senate are divided roughly as follows:
*   **37% Majoritarian (FPTP):** Single-member districts where the candidate with the most votes wins.
*   **61% Proportional (PR):** Multi-member districts where seats are allocated based on party lists.
*   **2% Overseas:** Reserved for Italians living abroad.

### The Single Ballot
Unlike Germany, you cannot split your vote. You cast **one vote** for a candidate in your district.
*   That vote counts for the candidate in the FPTP section.
*   It also automatically counts for the party (or coalition of parties) supporting that candidate in the Proportional section.

### Thresholds
*   **3%** for individual parties.
*   **10%** for coalitions of parties.

## 2. Illustrated Example: A District Election

**Scenario:** A district with **1 FPTP seat** and contributing to the **Proportional pool**.

### The Candidates
*   **Candidate Red:** Supported by the "Left Coalition" (Parties A and B).
*   **Candidate Blue:** Supported by the "Right Coalition" (Parties C, D, and E).
*   **Candidate Yellow:** Supported by an independent party (Party F).

### Voter Action
A voter marks the box for **Candidate Blue**.
1.  **Majoritarian Result:** Candidate Blue gets the voter's full support to win the local district seat.
2.  **Proportional Result:** The voter's vote is then divided among the parties in the Right Coalition (**C, D, and E**) based on their relative strength in that specific proportional district.

### If a Party fails the threshold:
If **Party E** only gets **0.5%** nationally:
*   Its votes are completely discarded.
*   If it gets **1.5%**, its votes are transferred to the other parties in its coalition (**C and D**).
*   If it gets **3%**, it keeps its own votes and gets its own seats.

---

## 3. Key Characteristics
*   **Coalition Focus:** The system is designed to force parties to form big alliances *before* the election. If you run alone (like Party F), you are likely to lose almost all the "winner-take-all" FPTP seats.
*   **Lack of Choice:** Since voters cannot choose specific candidates on the party lists (they are "closed"), the party leadership has a lot of control over who gets in.
*   **Instability:** While the system encourages alliances, these "marriage of convenience" coalitions often break apart once they are in government.
